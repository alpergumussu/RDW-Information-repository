Introduction

What is this
site?

This is an open educational masterclass on automotive cybersecurity — built for students of computer science, automotive engineering, and anyone curious about how modern vehicles can be attacked and defended.

Cars are no longer purely mechanical machines. A new vehicle today runs on dozens of networked computers, communicates wirelessly with manufacturers and infrastructure, and receives software updates over the air. That connectivity brings enormous capability — and an entirely new class of risk.

This masterclass gives you the language, the context, and the framework to understand that risk.

The material is structured as four self-contained modules. You can work through them in order for a complete picture, or jump directly to the topic most relevant to you.

What you will cover

Module 01 introduces the modern connected vehicle and its internal architecture. Module 02 examines how regulation is responding to the evolving threat landscape. Module 03 walks through the cybersecurity lifecycle from risk assessment to incident response. Module 04 looks ahead at emerging threats — from autonomous vehicles to AI and geopolitics.

Why it matters

Six reasons
this field exists.

Vehicle security is not a niche of IT security. The threat model, the consequences of failure, and the regulatory environment are all distinct.

01

Safety, not just data

A web breach loses passwords. A vehicle breach can move a steering rack. The blast radius includes the people in and around the car.

02

Long service life

Cars stay on the road for 12–20 years. Threat models written in 2018 must hold up against attackers with 2030's tools.

03

Heterogeneous code

Hundreds of suppliers. Code in C, Ada, Rust, Java, Linux user-space — every layer with its own vulnerability profile.

04

Always connected

5G modems, V2X radios, cloud APIs and mobile apps mean a fleet-wide remote attack surface that simply did not exist 15 years ago.

05

Privacy at scale

Telematics streams location, voice, biometrics, and behavior. The vehicle is one of the most data-rich devices a person owns.

06

Hard regulation

UNECE R155 makes a cybersecurity management system a precondition for type approval in 60+ markets. Compliance is no longer optional.

Vocabulary you'll meet

A short
field glossary.

Six terms used throughout the chapters. Hover any term in an article to see its definition again.

// term 01

ECU

Electronic Control Unit. A small embedded computer dedicated to one subsystem — engine, brakes, body, infotainment. A modern car has 70+.

// term 02

CAN bus

Controller Area Network. The shared 1980s broadcast bus most ECUs still talk over. No authentication built in. By design.

// term 03

Telematics

The cellular-and-cloud module that talks to the manufacturer for OTA updates, navigation, eCall, remote services, and stolen-vehicle recovery.

// term 04

OTA

Over-the-air update. Modern OTA pipelines push signed firmware to ECUs through the telematics module — for both features and security patches.

// term 05

RKE / PKE

Remote / Passive Keyless Entry. The encrypted radio between key fob and car. Every generation has been broken; every generation gets stronger.

// term 06

WP.29 R155

UN Regulation No. 155 from Working Party 29. Requires manufacturers to operate a cybersecurity management system across the entire vehicle lifecycle.
