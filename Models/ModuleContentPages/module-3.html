Introduction

In Module 1 you saw how connected vehicles became attackable. In Module 2 you saw how regulators responded. This module asks a different question: once a vehicle has been designed, approved, and sold, what happens to its security across the fifteen to twenty years that follow?

The short answer is that security cannot be a state — it has to be a process. A vehicle that was secure at launch is not secure at year ten unless someone has done deliberate, continuous work in the meantime. The frameworks that govern that work — ISO/SAE 21434, UN R155, UN R156, and increasingly the EU Cyber Resilience Act — are the subject of this module. They describe a lifecycle. The lifecycle, in practice, has gaps.

DOK Level 1 — Recall and Recognition

Security is not a feature you add at the end. At DOK 1 you are building the vocabulary of the lifecycle: the phases it contains, the frameworks that structure it, and the basic reasons why a vehicle's cybersecurity story does not begin or end at type approval.

Why a lifecycle approach is needed

A modern vehicle is sold once but operated for far longer than most consumer products. The average age of passenger cars on EU roads is currently around 12.5 years and has been rising for two decades — from 8.4 years in 2007 to 10.9 in 2013 to 12.5 in 2025. Typical lifespans vary by region: research cited by the European Environment Agency puts the average Western European car at around 18 years and the average Eastern European car at around 28 years. A vehicle bought in 2026 will, in many cases, still be operational in 2044.

Compare that with the typical lifespan of consumer software. A smartphone is supported for five to seven years; a laptop operating system for around ten. A vehicle's connected systems must remain secure for two to four times longer than that — against threats that have not been invented yet, using cryptographic algorithms that may no longer be considered safe, integrated with backend services that may no longer exist.

This timescale alone makes a one-off security assessment insufficient. A vehicle approved as secure in 2026 is not, by virtue of that approval, secure in 2036. The threat landscape will have changed. New vulnerabilities will have been discovered in components that were considered safe. Cryptographic primitives that were standard at design time may have been deprecated. The vehicle has not changed — but the environment around it has.

Why "secure at launch" is not enough

Three structural reasons drive the lifecycle approach.

Vulnerabilities are discovered, not designed. Every piece of non-trivial software contains vulnerabilities that nobody — not the developer, not the auditor, not the type approval authority — knew about at the time of release. They are found later, sometimes years later, by researchers, attackers, or accident. The CVE database lists tens of thousands of vulnerabilities discovered each year in software that was, at the time it shipped, considered to be in good order. A vehicle's software is no exception.

The attack surface keeps growing. Even if the vehicle's own software did not change, its environment does. New attack techniques are developed. New external interfaces (Wi-Fi standards, V2X protocols, charging networks) get added to the world the vehicle operates in. A vehicle designed before a particular attack class existed may be vulnerable to it in retrospect.

Software changes over time. OTA updates, app store extensions, navigation map updates, and even some hardware refreshes happen across a vehicle's life. Each change is a potential entry point for a new vulnerability and a potential break of a previously valid security assumption.

The combined effect is that the gap between a vehicle's actual security state and its security state at type approval grows over time. The lifecycle approach exists to close that gap continuously or, from a different perspective, to keep it from widening unmanageably.

How ISO/SAE 21434 and R155 structure the lifecycle

Module 2 introduced UN R155 (the regulation) and ISO/SAE 21434 (the engineering standard). Here we look at how they structure the vehicle lifecycle into phases, each with its own cybersecurity obligations.

ISO/SAE 21434 organises the lifecycle into five broad phases, with a set of continuous activities that run across all of them:

  • Concept phase — initial item definition, early TARA, derivation of cybersecurity goals and high-level requirements.
  • Product development — design, implementation, integration, verification, and validation of cybersecurity controls. Cybersecurity validation — the formal confirmation that the cybersecurity goals derived in concept have been achieved — sits at the end of this phase rather than as a phase of its own. This is where the V-model (covered at DOK 2) operates.
  • Production — secure handling of keys, firmware, and configuration during manufacturing. A vehicle assembled with the wrong keys, or with debug interfaces left enabled, is not secure regardless of what its design specified.
  • Operations and maintenance — the longest phase by far, containing two distinguishable activity sets. Operations covers what the manufacturer does to keep cybersecurity active in the field: monitoring threat intelligence, running anomaly detection, tracking new vulnerabilities, responding to incidents. Maintenance covers the corrective actions: OTA updates, patches, configuration changes, re-running TARAs against changed conditions. The two are related but not the same — and at DOK 2 you will see why the distinction matters.
  • End of cybersecurity support and decommissioning — the deliberate wind-down of security activity, communication to vehicle owners, and the question of what happens to data and credentials at the very end.

UN R155 maps onto these phases through the CSMS (Cybersecurity Management System), which the manufacturer must apply across all of them. R155 does not invent a different lifecycle — it requires the manufacturer to operate ISO/SAE 21434's lifecycle (or an equivalent) under a certified management system, with evidence at each phase that the work was done.

UN R156 sits alongside R155 and covers one specific lifecycle activity in detail: software updates. The Software Update Management System (SUMS) governs how changes to a vehicle's software are designed, validated, distributed, and tracked from initial release through end-of-life.

A vehicle outlives the moment of approval — and sometimes outlives more

Type approval certifies a vehicle at a moment in time. The lifecycle is everything that happens after that moment. Several things can happen across those decades that the moment-of-approval framing does not capture:

  • The OEM may discontinue the model line. Spare parts and software support may end before the vehicles themselves do.
  • Tier 1 or Tier 2 suppliers may go out of business, be acquired, or exit the automotive market. Components that the OEM does not own outright may become unsupportable.
  • Cellular networks supporting the vehicle's TCU may be decommissioned. The 2G and 3G shutdowns across Europe between 2020 and 2025 left a meaningful number of older connected vehicles without their original connectivity.
  • Ownership may change multiple times. The original buyer's relationship with the OEM is not necessarily inherited by the eighth owner buying a 14-year-old car from a small dealership.
  • Cryptographic standards may be deprecated. A vehicle that relies on SHA-1 or short RSA keys for any meaningful authentication step has a built-in expiry date that nobody put on the windscreen.

Each of these is part of the lifecycle picture. None of them are addressed by type approval. The lifecycle frameworks attempt to address some of them. Others remain genuinely unresolved, and we return to them at DOK 3.

The lifecycle phases in detail

The five phases introduced at DOK 1 are not equal in duration or in cybersecurity intensity. Concept and product development together may last two to four years for a new vehicle platform. Production may last another five to ten years across model years. Operations and maintenance is the dominant phase by time — fifteen years or more — and is where the lifecycle's most difficult cybersecurity work is done. Sitting alongside the phases, a set of continuous activities — vulnerability management, monitoring, and risk reassessment — runs across all of them and does not reduce to any single one.

Each phase has characteristic work products. The CSMS auditor and the type approval authority both look for evidence at the right phase, in the right form.

Cybersecurity validation — formal confirmation that the cybersecurity goals have been met — sits at the close of this phase. Cybersecurity requirements at each level; design specifications; verification reports; integration test results; validation report and cybersecurity case.

Reading this table (It's hidden on mobile phones) from left to right tells you what the manufacturer must do. Reading it from top to bottom shows you something else: the work compounds. A failure in concept-phase TARA produces vulnerabilities that show up in operations and maintenance years later. A failure in production key management may invalidate cryptographic protections that the design phase carefully specified.

The V-model

The V-model is a development framework borrowed from systems engineering and adapted for cybersecurity. It describes the relationship between phases of design (going down the left side of the V) and phases of verification (going up the right side). Each design phase has a corresponding verification phase that tests whether the design actually achieved what it claimed.

Reading the V from top-left, down, and back up the right:

  • Stakeholder requirements are defined at the top of the left arm. They are validated at the top of the right arm through acceptance testing — does the finished vehicle meet what stakeholders actually needed?
  • System cybersecurity requirements are derived next. They are verified by system-level cybersecurity testing — penetration testing, fuzzing, and red-team exercises against the integrated vehicle.
  • Architecture and component requirements follow. They are verified by integration testing — do the components, when assembled, behave securely as a system?
  • Detailed design and implementation sit at the bottom of the V. They are verified by unit-level testing — does each component meet its own cybersecurity specification?

The V-model exists because verification is most reliable when it directly targets a specification that was made at a comparable level of abstraction. Unit tests cannot tell you whether your stakeholder requirements were correct; acceptance testing cannot efficiently diagnose individual code bugs. Each level of design needs its own level of verification, and the V makes that relationship explicit.

For cybersecurity specifically, the V-model has one important consequence: the right-arm activities (testing, validation, acceptance) catch problems whose root causes are on the left arm (specification, design, requirements). A vulnerability discovered in penetration testing may originate in a system architecture decision made years earlier. Fixing it cleanly means going back up the left arm, not patching at the right. This is one reason why late-stage cybersecurity fixes are expensive: they often unravel design decisions whose costs were paid upstream and whose corrections must be re-paid downstream.

CSMS, SUMS, and Cyber Resilience as a lifecycle framework

Three regulatory instruments operate across the lifecycle simultaneously. They are not alternatives — they are layered.

The CSMS (UN R155)

The Cybersecurity Management System governs the manufacturer's organisational approach to cybersecurity across all phases. It is the meta-framework: the system that ensures the other systems exist and operate.

In lifecycle terms, the CSMS requires the manufacturer to demonstrate that:

  • Cybersecurity is integrated into governance at the organisational level, not delegated to a single team.
  • Risks are identified, assessed, treated, and reassessed across all phases — not just at concept.
  • Verification and testing happen at the right phases with the right evidence.
  • Monitoring continues into operations and maintenance, with response processes for issues discovered after release.
  • Supplier management extends cybersecurity requirements down the supply chain.

The CSMS certificate is valid for a maximum of three years and must be renewed. This forced periodic re-audit is itself a lifecycle mechanism: it means the manufacturer's processes are re-examined while the vehicles produced under previous certificates are still on the road.

The SUMS (UN R156)

The Software Update Management System governs how changes to vehicle software are managed across the lifecycle. It is the operational arm of the lifecycle for everything that happens after a vehicle leaves the factory and receives software updates.

Where the CSMS asks "do you have a system for managing cybersecurity?", the SUMS asks "do you have a system for managing change?". The two are deeply linked. An OTA update that fixes a vulnerability is simultaneously a CSMS activity (responding to identified risk) and a SUMS activity (managing a software change with type approval implications).

The SUMS includes records that span the lifecycle: for any given vehicle, the manufacturer must be able to answer what software was installed on what date, who authorised the update, what the impact assessment said, and what the verification produced. Years later, these records are what allow forensic reconstruction of which vehicles were affected by a discovered vulnerability.

The EU Cyber Resilience Act (CRA)

The Cyber Resilience Act, which entered into force in 2024 with full application from late 2027, is horizontal EU legislation covering cybersecurity of products with digital elements. It does not target the vehicle itself — that is R155's territory — but it applies to many components that go into a vehicle, and to the aftermarket products that vehicles connect with.

The CRA introduces lifecycle obligations of its own. Manufacturers of products with digital elements must:

  • Provide security updates for a defined support period (initially proposed as five years, with flexibility for products designed for longer use).
  • Report actively exploited vulnerabilities to ENISA within 24 hours of becoming aware and provide a follow-up notification within 72 hours.
  • Maintain a Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) so that components and their dependencies are traceable.

The CRA's significance for automotive is that it formalises lifecycle obligations that were previously industry-specific or voluntary and applies them to the supply chain. A Tier 2 sensor manufacturer that previously had no direct regulatory obligation to maintain security updates may now have one under the CRA — and that obligation flows up to the Tier 1 and OEM that depend on the component.

The three instruments operate at different levels: the CSMS at the organisational level, the SUMS at the change-management level, and the CRA at the product-component level. Together they form a lifecycle framework that no single instrument provides alone.

TARA as a recurring process, not a one-off

In Module 2 you encountered TARA — Threat Analysis and Risk Assessment — as the structured methodology by which manufacturers identify and assess cybersecurity risks. At that point we treated it largely as a concept-phase activity: the assessment that feeds initial cybersecurity goals.

In lifecycle terms, that framing is incomplete. TARA is not done once. It is re-run whenever the conditions on which the previous assessment was based have changed materially. The CSMS requires this; ISO/SAE 21434 specifies it; and good engineering practice would do it anyway.

Potential triggers for re-running a TARA include:

  • A new vulnerability is discovered in a component the TARA had assumed was safe.
  • A new attack technique is published that affects a class of systems present in the vehicle.
  • A planned OTA update changes the vehicle's behaviour, architecture, or attack surface.
  • A supplier change introduces components whose threat profile differs from the original.
  • Field data — anomaly reports, incident response output, monitoring telemetry — shows a previously unrecognised pattern of attack or attempted attack.
  • Cryptographic primitives or protocols used by the vehicle are deprecated or shown to be weak.

Each re-assessment produces a delta: what is now different about the risk profile, and what mitigations (if any) need to change in response. The records of these re-assessments accumulate across the vehicle's life. They are part of what an auditor reviews at CSMS renewal, and what a regulator may demand if a vehicle is implicated in an incident.

The practical consequence is that an OEM's TARA library for a single vehicle platform may contain dozens of related assessments by the time the platform is several years old. The discipline is not in producing the first one — it is in keeping them coherent over time, so that the assumptions in one assessment can be traced through to the conclusions of another, and the cumulative picture remains legible.

Responsibility split between OEM, Tier 1, and Tier 2

The lifecycle is not the work of a single organisation. It is divided across the supply chain, and the division is uneven by design.

At a structural level:

  • The OEM is responsible to the regulator. It holds the CSMS certificate and the per-vehicle-type cybersecurity type approval. From the type approval authority's perspective, the OEM is the responsible party for everything that happens in the vehicle, including the parts supplied by others.
  • Tier 1 suppliers are responsible to the OEM. They deliver complete systems — infotainment platforms, ADAS modules, body controllers, gateways. Their contractual obligations include cybersecurity work products: their own TARAs for the components they supply, evidence of secure development, vulnerability response commitments, and update mechanisms compatible with the OEM's SUMS.
  • Tier 2 suppliers are responsible to the Tier 1. They supply the components inside the Tier 1's systems — semiconductors, sensors, communication modules, software libraries. Their cybersecurity obligations are largely defined by the Tier 1 contract, with the CRA increasingly imposing direct regulatory obligations on top.

In lifecycle terms, this means that operations-and-maintenance work is also distributed. A vulnerability discovered in field operation may need:

  • The OEM to triage the issue, assess type approval impact, and notify the regulator.
  • The Tier 1 to identify which component is affected and develop a fix.
  • The Tier 2 to provide an updated component, patch, or workaround.
  • The OEM to package and deliver the fix via SUMS-governed OTA, retaining records of which vehicles received it.

If any link in that chain is missing — the Tier 2 has discontinued the component, the Tier 1 no longer has the engineering capacity, the OEM's OTA mechanism does not reach a particular generation of vehicle — the fix may not happen, even though all three parties accept that it should. The chain is contractual; the chain is also physical and economic, and economics does not always cooperate with the contract. We return to this at DOK 3.

Q-02

CSMS and SUMS overlap significantly when an OTA update fixes a security vulnerability. What does each instrument require in that situation, and where would gaps appear if only one of them were in place?

flip for answer

Answer

CSMS and SUMS overlap. The CSMS requires that the manufacturer identify the vulnerability, assess risk, and decide on a treatment — the cybersecurity decision-making. The SUMS requires that the resulting software change be designed, validated, distributed, and recorded — the change-management mechanics. Without the CSMS, the change has no governance behind it (why was this update issued and is it the right one?). Without the SUMS, the decision has no reliable mechanism for reaching the vehicle (where did the update go, did it install, what is the new RxSWIN?). They are interlocked: each requires the other to be operationally complete.

DOK Level 3 — Strategic Thinking

At DOK 1 you mapped the phases. At DOK 2 you mapped the work inside them. This section asks what happens when the lifecycle is stressed — when timelines compress, suppliers fail, vehicles change hands repeatedly, and the boundary between "still supported" and "effectively abandoned" becomes a matter of judgment rather than policy. None of these questions have settled answers. You are working with the same incomplete information that practitioners in this field work with.

Supply chain continuity when a supplier fails

The lifecycle frameworks assume that the supply chain remains in place across the years a vehicle is supported. Tier 1s continue trading, Tier 2s continue producing, and components remain available either as original parts or as compatible replacements. The CSMS and the contractual chain are designed around that assumption.

In practice the assumption breaks regularly. Suppliers go bankrupt, are acquired, exit the automotive market, or simply discontinue product lines that the OEM still depends on. The 2020s alone saw notable consolidations across the automotive semiconductor industry, the infotainment platform space, and the cellular module market. Each consolidation closed product lines and ended support contracts that had been counted on.

Consider a concrete scenario. An OEM ships a vehicle in 2022 with a TCU containing a cellular modem from a specific Tier 2 supplier. In 2027, that supplier is acquired by a larger company that decides to discontinue the product line. The Tier 1 that integrates the TCU still has stock for some years but no engineering support for new firmware. In 2029, a vulnerability is published in that modem's baseband firmware. The OEM's CSMS requires a response. The Tier 1 has no fix. The Tier 2 no longer exists in a form that can produce one.

What should the OEM do? The options are all unsatisfactory:

  • Reverse-engineer the modem firmware and produce a fix in-house. Likely outside the OEM's competence and possibly outside what the licence terms with the (now-defunct) supplier permitted.
  • Compensate by hardening other layers. Tighten gateway filtering, restrict what the TCU can do, accept that the baseband vulnerability remains but limit what it can be used to reach. A reasonable mitigation but not a fix.
  • Issue a recall for hardware replacement. Possible but extremely expensive, and requires a replacement part that may not exist.
  • Disable the affected functionality. Possibly turning off remote services, eCall, or OTA capability itself. The vehicle becomes less of a connected vehicle, which is a degradation owners did not sign up for.
  • Accept the risk and document the rationale. Formally acceptable under CSMS if properly justified, but uncomfortable territory if the vulnerability is severe and the vehicle population is large.

The CSMS requires the OEM to make a decision and document it. It does not provide a mechanism that fixes the underlying problem: a piece of the supply chain on which the vehicle's security depends no longer exists. The framework assumes continuity that the market does not guarantee.

The deeper question is whether the framework should require something different upstream — perhaps mandated escrow of firmware source code and signing keys, perhaps required engineering succession plans, perhaps regulator-funded support arrangements for orphaned components. Each of those interventions has costs and consequences of its own, and none is currently in place at the regulatory level.

End of Life and End of Support: two dates that are often confused

The lifecycle ends, eventually — but it ends twice, and the two endings are not the same. The vocabulary matters because the obligations attached to each are different, and conflating them produces a misleading picture of when a manufacturer's cybersecurity duties actually finish.

End of Life (EOL) is the point at which the item is no longer being produced or sold. The manufacturing line for that vehicle model has closed. Spare parts may still be available; vehicles already produced are still in active operation in the field. EOL is a production decision, not a cybersecurity decision. Crucially, ISO/SAE 21434 cybersecurity obligations may still apply during the period after EOL, because vehicles remain on the road for 10–15 or more years after production ends.

End of Support (EOS) is the point at which the manufacturer formally declares that it will no longer provide cybersecurity support — patches, updates, monitoring — for the item. ISO/SAE 21434 requires this declaration to be explicitly defined and communicated to all relevant stakeholders. EOS is the cybersecurity decision, and it has direct regulatory consequences: after EOS, the operational and maintenance obligations associated with that vehicle population end.

EOL and EOS do not necessarily coincide. A vehicle model may go out of production years before its cybersecurity support window closes. A common pattern looks like this: production ends in 2030 (EOL), but the manufacturer commits to ten further years of cybersecurity support, declaring EOS for 2040. During the EOL-to-EOS gap, vehicles are no longer being built, but the manufacturer must still monitor for vulnerabilities, issue OTA updates, and respond to incidents — under both R155's CSMS and R156's SUMS.

With the distinction in place, the questions that follow become sharper.

Whose decision is EOS?

The OEM decides, in practice. ISO/SAE 21434 requires the declaration to be explicit and communicated to stakeholders, but it does not set a minimum date. The CRA, applied to component manufacturers, proposed a minimum support period (five years was the initial figure, with discretion to extend for products with longer expected use). Automotive practice in major markets typically commits to longer support — ten or fifteen years from EOL for some OEMs — but this is policy, not regulation.

Owners are informed of EOS but have no veto. This is reasonable from an industrial perspective — the OEM bears the cost — and uncomfortable from an ownership perspective. A vehicle that is still functional, still on the road, still being driven by someone who paid for it, may be declared cybersecurity-unsupported by a decision in which the owner had no say. The EOL date is irrelevant to that owner; the EOS date is what determines whether their vehicle will receive a patch when the next vulnerability lands.

What does EOS actually mean in practice?

The term EOS is doing a lot of work. Different OEMs draw the line at different points, and ISO/SAE 21434 requires the declaration to be defined but does not specify what it must contain. EOS can mean any of the following:

  • No new feature updates, but security patches continue.
  • No new security patches, but the OTA infrastructure continues to function.
  • Neither updates nor backend services — the vehicle is no longer a connected vehicle in the OEM's eyes.
  • Active termination — keys revoked, backend connections refused, eCall services withdrawn.

Two OEMs can both legitimately use the term EOS to mean meaningfully different things. The owner reading the warranty booklet rarely catches the distinction. The standard requires the manufacturer to declare what EOS covers; it does not require the declaration to be comparable across manufacturers.

What happens to data and credentials between EOL and EOS — and after?

Between EOL and EOS, the vehicle is in a state that is well-defined regulatorily but commercially awkward: still supported, no longer being produced, often quietly deprioritized by the OEM whose engineering attention has moved to newer models. Cybersecurity obligations remain in force. Whether they are fulfilled with the same rigor as for current production vehicles is an empirical question, and one that CSMS auditors have to actively examine rather than assume.

After EOS, the situation is different. A past EOS vehicle still holds cryptographic keys, may still contain personal data in its infotainment system, may still be associated with the original owner's accounts at the OEM. Few frameworks address this cleanly: should keys be revoked centrally at EOS? Should the vehicle be decommissioned in a positive sense — wiped, disconnected, formally retired? In practice, the vehicle simply continues, perhaps in the hands of an owner several rungs down the resale chain, still containing whatever it contained when EOS was declared, and possibly still connecting to backend services that the OEM has wound down or repurposed.

And what about second-hand owners?

EOS policy is almost always written from the first owner's perspective. The owner who buys a vehicle new and trades it in after seven years has experienced a different lifecycle than the owner who buys the same vehicle at fourteen years old from a small lot. The second owner's relationship with the OEM is often non-existent. The information about update history, EOL date, EOS date, and known vulnerabilities is in the OEM's system, not in the vehicle's documentation. Many second-hand buyers do not know what they have bought, from a cybersecurity perspective, and have no easy way to find out — including whether the vehicle has already passed EOS.

This is one of the genuinely under-addressed problems in the lifecycle framework. R155 governs the OEM's process. R156 governs the update mechanism. Neither has a strong instrument for the question of how cybersecurity status — including EOL and EOS dates — should follow the vehicle as it changes hands across two decades.

Strategic implications

The supply chain continuity problem and the EOS problem are not separate. They are different faces of the same structural condition: regulatory frameworks address what the OEM should do, but a vehicle's lifecycle is longer than any single party's reliable engagement with it. Suppliers exit faster than vehicles wear out. OEMs declare EOS before vehicles end use. Owners change more often than software does. The system assumed continuity that the underlying market does not produce.

There are several broad directions reform might take. Each involves trade-offs and none is uncontroversial.

  • Mandated longer EOS windows. Forces OEMs to plan for longer post-EOL support but raises the price of new vehicles and may discourage feature investment.
  • Escrow and succession requirements. Require suppliers to deposit critical IP — firmware source, signing keys, design documentation — into escrow that becomes accessible if the supplier fails. Increases costs and creates IP-protection complexity, but materially improves continuity, particularly across the EOL-to-EOS gap.
  • A second-market cybersecurity transparency regime. Require that cybersecurity status — update history, known vulnerabilities, EOL date, EOS date — be disclosed at every vehicle sale. Closer to consumer-protection regulation than to current automotive practice.
  • A regulated post-EOS decommissioning protocol — formal retirement of the vehicle's connected functions at or after EOS, central key revocation, defined wind-down of backend services. Closer to the regulatory model used for aircraft retirement than to anything currently in automotive practice.

These are not technical questions. They are policy questions about how risk and cost should be distributed between manufacturers, suppliers, owners, and the public. The DOK 3 questions below ask you to reason through some of them. There are no correct answers — only better and worse-reasoned ones.

DOK Level 4 — Extended Thinking

You have reached the end of the lifecycle — which is also, in a connected vehicle, the beginning of the next one. What follows are not questions about process or compliance. They are questions about what it means to be responsible for a system that will outlast your engagement with it, your organization’s engagement with it, and possibly the regulatory framework that governed it at the start.

Open problem: stewardship across institutional time

A vehicle on the road in 2044 was designed in 2024. The engineers who designed it are, on average, no longer at the company that built it. The regulator who approved it may have been reorganized, merged, and renamed. The cryptographic algorithms it depends on may be deprecated. The supplier base that produced its components has, in part, ceased to exist. The framework that governed its cybersecurity at launch — UN R155 — has had at least one round of substantial revision, possibly more.

Every party with a contractual or institutional duty toward that vehicle's cybersecurity has, in some sense, moved on. The vehicle has not.

This is not a unique feature of automotive. Aircraft remain in service for thirty to forty years; some military equipment lasts longer. What is unique is that none of those long-lived systems have, until recently, been continuously connected to networks they did not control, exposed to actively evolving adversaries, and dependent on cryptographic protections that have known expiry conditions.

The lifecycle frameworks we have are organizational frameworks. They specify what manufacturers, suppliers, and regulators should do during the period in which they remain meaningfully engaged. They do not specify what happens to security responsibility once that engagement effectively ends — neither because the support period is over (that is at least defined) nor because the relevant party has dissolved, been absorbed, or simply lost the institutional memory required to act.

This raises a question that the regulatory framework has not yet answered: what does it mean to be responsible for the security of a system whose operational life exceeds the institutional life of the parties responsible for it?

There are several broad models the answer might take, each with deep consequences.

  • The retirement model. At EOS, the vehicle is formally decommissioned from a connected-systems perspective — keys revoked, backend disconnection enforced, V2X participation disabled. The vehicle continues to exist as a vehicle but ceases to exist as a connected vehicle. This mirrors aviation, where aircraft are formally retired from registers and their type certificates closed. It requires a regulatory infrastructure capable of executing the retirement, and an owner population willing to accept that their vehicle has been substantially altered without their decision.
  • The transfer model. Cybersecurity responsibility is transferred at EOS to a designated successor — perhaps an independent industry body, perhaps a regulator-administered fund, perhaps the owner of the vehicle. Each has problems. Industry bodies need funding and a mandate they currently do not have. Regulators are not engineering organisations. Owners are not security professionals, and most cannot reasonably be expected to maintain their own vehicle's firmware.
  • The acceptance model. Society accepts that some level of cybersecurity degradation is inevitable in older connected vehicles, and manages the consequences through other mechanisms — insurance, road regulation, possibly access restrictions to certain infrastructure (urban areas, autonomous-vehicle corridors). The vehicle remains in use but is implicitly understood to be a different kind of risk than a newer one. This is closer to how we treat older vehicles for emissions and active-safety equipment today, where PTI-style inspections gradually disqualify them from certain uses.
  • The collective stewardship model. Cybersecurity for connected vehicles past EOS becomes a publicly-funded function — not because the OEM has failed, but because the vehicles are now part of public infrastructure in a way that no longer fits a purely private support model. The argument is that the externalities of insecure vehicles fall on the public: a compromised vehicle disrupts traffic, interferes with V2X infrastructure, contributes to cascading incidents, and creates safety risks for road users who never bought the vehicle. If the public bears the cost of the risk, there is a case for the public bearing the cost of mitigation once the original manufacturer has, by agreement, stepped away.

None of these models is currently in operation. None has been seriously debated at the regulatory level in the way that pre-market cybersecurity has been debated. The DOK 4 questions below sit in this territory.

Open problem: cryptographic deprecation across the lifecycle

A second open problem deserves naming separately, because it is one of the few in this masterclass with a known schedule. The cryptographic algorithms that currently protect vehicle communications, OTA updates, and stored credentials are not permanent. Specific deprecation timelines are visible:

  • SHA-1 has been considered weak since the mid-2010s and is being removed from active use across IT systems. Some automotive systems still depend on it in legacy paths.
  • RSA with smaller key sizes (1024-bit, and increasingly 2048-bit for long-lived signatures) is approaching the end of its trusted life within current best-practice horizons.
  • Conventional public-key cryptography overall — RSA, elliptic-curve cryptography (ECC) — is at risk from sufficiently large quantum computers. Estimates of when such machines will exist vary widely, but the post-quantum cryptography (PQC) transition is being actively standardised by NIST and others, with the first PQC standards published in 2024.

The lifecycle problem is that a vehicle designed today, with current cryptography, will still be on the road when at least some of these transitions have happened. A vehicle whose OTA signing keys are RSA-2048 in 2026 may be in operation in 2042 with signing keys that would now be considered marginal. A vehicle whose V2X messages are signed with current ECC may, by then, be exchanging messages with a V2X ecosystem that has moved to PQC.

The technical mitigation — crypto agility, the ability to update cryptographic primitives via software — is well understood and increasingly required by standards. The operational mitigation — actually performing the migration across a fielded vehicle population over the years available — is much less well understood. It is one of the largest open lifecycle problems and one of the few with a forced timeline.

The synthesis prompt

This is the DOK 4 exercise. The questions that follow it are shorter and more bounded; this is where the heaviest reasoning happens.

The question is:

Design a cybersecurity lifecycle framework (or a specific extension to the current framework) that addresses the stewardship problem identified above. Your proposal must work for vehicles that will be in operation in 2045 and beyond, designed and approved under conditions that may no longer apply.

Your proposal should:

  • Identify the specific aspect of the stewardship problem the proposal addresses."Long-term security" is not a problem; organizational discontinuity, cryptographic deprecation, and orphaned components are. Name what you are solving.
  • Reference the existing framework. Where does your proposal sit in relation to R155, R156, ISO/SAE 21434, the CRA? Is it an extension, a replacement, a complement? Frameworks built without reference to what already exists tend not to survive contact with regulatory reality.
  • Specify what changes — institutionally, contractually, technically. Who does something they are not currently doing? Who pays? Who has authority they do not currently have? "Better coordination" is not a proposal.
  • Identify the stakeholders whose interests are in tension, and how your proposal navigates them. OEMs, suppliers, regulators, owners, second-hand buyers, the public. A proposal that helps everyone equally is suspicious; most worthwhile policy distributes costs and benefits unevenly and acknowledges the trade-off.
  • State what your proposal does not solve. There is no proposal that closes every gap. Naming the residual problem honestly is a sign of analytical maturity.

The format is one you might find in a working group submission, a regulatory consultation, or an industry position paper. What matters is the reasoning, not the institutional polish.
