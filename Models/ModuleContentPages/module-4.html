DOK Level 1 — Recall and Recognition

This module is about the future — where the connected vehicle ecosystem is heading, what it is becoming part of, and what that means for the people inside it. At DOK 1 you are building the context needed for everything that follows: what capabilities already exist, who holds them, and what questions they raise. No analysis is required yet — read to recognise and remember.

The expanding attack surface

The connected vehicle does not exist in isolation. The same connectivity that enables remote diagnostics, OTA updates, and navigation makes the vehicle a node in a much larger networked ecosystem — and that ecosystem is expanding.

Autonomous taxis, delivery drones, logistics bots, and commercial vessels are all converging on the same architectural pattern: software-defined, network-connected, remotely manageable platforms. The cybersecurity principles that apply to a passenger car apply equally to an autonomous delivery van, a drone operating in urban airspace, or a vessel using AIS (Automatic identification system) for navigation. The attack surface is no longer the vehicle — it is the entire connected mobility ecosystem.

Remote capabilities — who holds the keys

Modern connected vehicles support a range of remote functions that did not exist a decade ago:

  • Remote diagnostics — the manufacturer can read fault codes, sensor data, and system logs without the vehicle being present at a workshop
  • Driving behaviour data — speed, acceleration, braking force, cornering, and location history are continuously collected and transmitted to OEM backends, insurers, or fleet operators
  • Cybersecurity monitoring — anomaly detection systems report suspicious behaviour to backend platforms for analysis
  • Remote disable — some vehicles can be remotely immobilised by the manufacturer, fleet operator, or in some jurisdictions, law enforcement

Each of these capabilities has a legitimate purpose. Each is also a capability that someone other than the driver holds. The driver may not know which are active, who has access to the data they generate, or under what conditions remote disable can be invoked.

This is not a hypothetical concern. It is the current operational reality of most new vehicles sold in Europe.

Privacy implications

A connected vehicle is a continuous surveillance platform. It knows where you are, where you have been, how you drive, who you call, what music you listen to, and when you leave home in the morning. That data is collected, transmitted, stored, and in many cases shared with third parties — insurers, navigation providers, advertising platforms, and government authorities — under terms most drivers have never read.

GDPR applies. The Data Act applies. In practice, enforcement is uneven and the data flows are opaque. Most drivers have no meaningful visibility into what their vehicle knows about them.

Ethical implications

The connected vehicle raises ethical questions the industry has not answered publicly. Who decides what data is collected? Who decides who receives it? Who decides whether the vehicle can be remotely disabled? Not the driver.

These are not technical questions. They are questions about power, consent, and the relationship between the individual and the systems they depend on.

The technology to act on that data is not theoretical — it is already partially deployed. Dynamic insurance pricing based on real-time driving behavior is live in several European markets (Usage-Based Insurance or UBI). A driver who brakes sharply, corners fast, or drives late at night pays more — automatically, based on data their vehicle generates and transmits without their active involvement.

The logical extensions are not far behind. Automatic fines issued without speed cameras, based on GPS and accelerometer data. Congestion charges calculated from continuous location tracking. Insurance coverage suspended in real time if driving behavior crosses a threshold. Each of these applications has a policy rationale.

Each requires that the vehicle continuously monitors, records, and reports on the behavior of its occupant. The capability that makes remote diagnostics possible is the same capability that makes all of the above possible. What separates them is not technology — it is governance. And governance can change.

The shopping cart dilemma

There is a well-known informal test of civic character: the shopping cart. There is no law requiring you to return it. No enforcement, no penalty. People who return it do so because they have internalized a norm of consideration for others.

The relevance to connected vehicles is this: the regulatory frameworks governing vehicle cybersecurity — R155, R156, mandatory ADAS systems, data collection requirements — exist partly because not everyone returns the shopping cart. Manufacturers who would voluntarily implement strong cybersecurity do not need R155. It exists for the ones who would not.

The problem is what happens next. Once voluntary compliance fails and regulation fills the gap, regulation becomes the floor — and the floor rises. Each mandatory safety system adds software complexity. Each software component adds attack surface. Each attack surface justifies additional regulation. The individual driver, who never asked for any of it, inherits both the benefits and the constraints — without ever being consulted.

The proportions are not equal. The consumer who leaves their cart costs someone a short walk. The manufacturer who leaves their systems unsecured, who collects and monetizes driving behavior without meaningful consent, who implements remote disable without disclosure — that choice lands on millions of people simultaneously, without their knowledge, with consequences that range from financial to physical.

Same dilemma. Same moral structure. Entirely different scale.

The driver of a modern connected vehicle has not been given that choice. The data collection was in the terms of service. The remote disable capability was in the type approval. The mandatory ADAS systems were required by regulation before the vehicle reached the showroom. The driver accepted all of it by purchasing the vehicle — but the alternatives were a used car with no connectivity, or no car at all.

Individual sovereignty — the principle that a person is the primary authority over themselves and their property — requires meaningful alternatives. When the regulatory and commercial environment systematically eliminates those alternatives, the formal existence of choice does not constitute meaningful consent.

The shopping cart dilemma assumes you could have left the cart where it stood. In the connected vehicle, that option has already been removed. The question is not whether you return it. The question is who decided you had to take it in the first place — and whether anyone thought to ask you.

UBI uses real-time driving behaviour — speed, braking force, cornering, time of day — transmitted by the vehicle's own sensors and telematics to calculate insurance pricing dynamically. The right existing and the right being meaningful are not the same thing.

DOK Level 2 — Skills and Concepts

At DOK 1 you saw what the ecosystem looks like and who controls it. At DOK 2 you examine where it breaks under pressure. These passages look at how existing safety and inspection frameworks fail to account for software-defined systems, how machine learning introduces failure modes that traditional engineering was not built to handle, and how the dependencies that make connected vehicles useful become liabilities when they are unavailable or under attack. You are expected to understand how these mechanisms work and draw connections between them.

Implications for PTI

Periodic Technical Inspection — the MOT in the UK, APK in the Netherlands — was designed for mechanical systems. An inspector checks brakes, lights, emissions, and structural integrity. They do not check whether the vehicle's software has been tampered with, whether the OTA update history is intact, or whether the ECU firmware matches what the manufacturer originally shipped.

As vehicles become software-defined, the gap between what PTI checks and what actually determines vehicle safety grows. A vehicle can pass every mechanical check and still be running compromised firmware on its braking ECU.

This creates a genuine regulatory gap. R155 requires manufacturers to implement cybersecurity management systems. It does not require periodic verification that those systems remain intact throughout the vehicle's operational life. PTI, as currently structured, cannot fill that gap — the tools, training, and standards do not exist yet.

The question of who is responsible for verifying the ongoing cybersecurity integrity of a vehicle in service is unresolved. It sits between the manufacturer, the type approval authority, and the periodic inspection regime — and currently falls through the gaps between all three.

AI as an attack surface

Artificial intelligence is increasingly embedded in vehicle systems — not as a novelty but as a core functional component. Object detection, trajectory planning, driver monitoring, and anomaly detection all rely on machine learning models. These models have properties that traditional software does not.

A traditional software function either works or it does not. An ML model works on average, across a distribution of inputs it was trained on. It can fail silently on inputs outside that distribution — and those failures can be systematic, predictable, and exploitable.

ISO PAS 21448 — Safety of the Intended Functionality (SOTIF) — addresses this directly. SOTIF was developed because ISO 26262 (functional safety) was designed for deterministic systems. A sensor that fails has a known failure mode. An ML model that misclassifies a pedestrian because of unusual lighting conditions, an adversarial sticker, or a distribution shift does not. SOTIF requires manufacturers to identify and mitigate insufficiencies in intended functionality — not just hardware failures.

The security implication is that adversarial attacks on ML perception systems are not just safety issues — they are SOTIF issues. An attacker who can reliably trigger a misclassification has found a vulnerability that exists by design in the model's decision boundary, not in the code that implements it.

Graceful degradation

A safety-critical system should fail safely. In automotive engineering this is called graceful degradation — the principle that when a component fails, the system should degrade to a safe state rather than failing catastrophically.

In a connected vehicle, graceful degradation has a cybersecurity dimension. What happens when the cellular connection drops? What happens when the OTA server is unreachable? What happens when the V2X infrastructure is unavailable or actively malicious?

A vehicle that cannot function safely without connectivity is a vehicle that can be rendered unsafe by denying connectivity. A vehicle that trusts V2X messages without fallback verification can be manipulated by whoever controls the infrastructure. Graceful degradation requires that safety-critical functions remain available and correct in the absence of external inputs — and that the vehicle can detect when external inputs are inconsistent with its own sensor data.

This is harder than it sounds. Modern ADAS functions increasingly depend on map data, V2X inputs, and cloud-based processing. Designing systems that remain safe when those dependencies are unavailable or compromised requires making explicit which functions are core and which are augmentative — a distinction the industry does not always make clearly.

Infrastructure collapse and cascading effects

Connected vehicles are part of infrastructure. They depend on cellular networks, cloud backends, GPS satellites, and V2X roadside units. They also feed into infrastructure — traffic management systems, emergency response coordination, toll collection, and increasingly the power grid via V2G.

A large-scale failure in any of these dependencies cascades. A cellular network outage affects OTA delivery, remote diagnostics, eCall, and telematics simultaneously. A GPS spoofing attack affects navigation, telematics timestamping, and in some implementations V2X positioning. A compromised traffic management system that pushes false data to V2X infrastructure affects every vehicle in its coverage area.

These failures do not have to be accidental. A deliberate, coordinated attack on the systems that connected vehicles depend on is not a different category of threat — it is the same architecture, targeted intentionally. The difference between a network outage and a network attack is intent, not impact. And unlike a power grid or a pipeline, a vehicle fleet is not a single target. It is millions of individually addressable endpoints, each with its own software stack, each reachable over cellular — and each physically present in the world.

The 2023 film Leave the World Behind depicted a version of this scenario: autonomous vehicles receiving corrupted instructions and converging on a highway simultaneously, blocking roads across a region and rendering highways inaccessible. It was fiction but the architectural conditions that would make it possible are not.

Q-02

A vehicle's automatic emergency braking relies on V2X input to detect hazards beyond sensor range. The V2X infrastructure in the area is taken offline by a denial-of-service attack. Should the vehicle continue operating? What does graceful degradation require in this scenario?

flip for answer

Answer

The vehicle should continue operating — stopping on a motorway because V2X went offline would itself be unsafe. Graceful degradation requires that safety-critical functions fall back to sensor-only operation when external inputs are unavailable, that the system detects the loss of V2X and adjusts its confidence accordingly, and that it does not treat the absence of a hazard signal as confirmation that no hazard exists. The V2X input is augmentative, not foundational. If the system was designed with V2X as a dependency rather than an enhancement, graceful degradation has already failed at the design stage.

Q-03

The Colonial Pipeline attack caused fuel shortages across the US east coast from a single compromised network. What is the automotive equivalent of that single point of failure, and what would a comparable attack look like in practice?

flip for answer

Answer

The automotive equivalent of a single point of failure is the OTA update infrastructure — a central signing and distribution system that pushes software to vehicles across an entire fleet. A comparable attack would compromise the update pipeline, sign a malicious firmware package with a stolen or forged key, and distribute it to vehicles via their normal update channel. Unlike the pipeline, which had one physical chokepoint, the automotive attack surface is distributed across millions of endpoints — each one executing the malicious update independently, with no central point at which the spread can be stopped once distribution begins.

Q-04

The Leave the World Behind scenario — vehicles converging on a highway simultaneously — is described as fiction whose architectural conditions are not. What specific architectural properties of connected vehicles would make that scenario technically plausible?

flip for answer

Answer

The scenario requires: vehicles that receive and act on external commands without requiring driver confirmation; a shared communication channel that reaches multiple vehicles simultaneously; insufficient authentication of command sources; and no safe fallback when commands are inconsistent with sensor data. All four of these conditions exist in current connected vehicle architectures. The V2X broadcast model, the absence of robust misbehaviour detection at scale, and the increasing automation of vehicle responses to external inputs are the architectural properties that make the scenario technically plausible — not hypothetically, but as a consequence of design decisions already in production.

DOK 3 — Strategic Thinking

At DOK 2 you examined where systems break. At DOK 3 the question becomes: who is responsible when those failures cross borders, jurisdictions, and sectors — and who should be? These passages ask you to reason about supply chain trust, institutional coordination, and the governance gaps that exist between organisations and nations. There are no clean answers. You are expected to evaluate trade-offs, recognise where reasonable positions differ, and justify your thinking from incomplete information.

V2X at scale — from warning system to operational infrastructure

Current V2X deployment is a preview, not the finished picture. Most vehicles that support V2X today receive messages and present them to the driver as advisory information — a warning that a vehicle ahead has braked hard, a notification that a traffic light is about to change. The driver decides what to do with that information. The vehicle does not act on it autonomously.

That is changing. As SAE automation levels rise and 5G-based C-V2X infrastructure matures, V2X transitions from an information channel into an operational input — messages the vehicle acts on directly, without human intermediation. Platooning systems where trucks maintain ample spacing based on V2X coordination. Intersection management where vehicles negotiate right of way with traffic infrastructure rather than reading a light. Emergency vehicle corridors cleared automatically as a priority signal propagates through a fleet.

Each of these applications is technically feasible today. Each requires that vehicles trust V2X messages enough to act on them. And that trust, once extended, cannot easily be withdrawn on a message-by-message basis in real time.

The infrastructure supporting this is not neutral. Roadside units are owned and operated by somebody — municipalities, national road authorities, private operators, or combinations of all three. The certificate authorities that sign V2X messages sit inside PKI hierarchies that are governed at national or regional level. The 5G networks that carry C-V2X traffic are operated by commercial entities under national licensing regimes.

As V2X scales, the question of who controls that infrastructure becomes a question of who influences vehicle behaviour at national scale. A compromised roadside unit with valid credentials does not just affect one vehicle — it affects every vehicle in its broadcast range that is configured to act on operational V2X input. A certificate authority that issues credentials to a malicious actor gives that actor a trusted voice in the V2X ecosystem across every jurisdiction that recognises that CA.

This is not the same threat model as hacking an individual vehicle. It is closer to the threat model of compromising a traffic management centre — except the consequences propagate not through signal timing but through the behaviour of every automated vehicle in range, simultaneously, without any human operator in the loop to notice something is wrong.

The governance frameworks that currently exist — R155, IEEE 1609, the ETSI C-ITS certificate framework — were designed for a V2X ecosystem where vehicles receive advisory information. They were not designed for one where vehicles act on operational commands from infrastructure they cannot physically verify. That gap will need to close before V2X deployment reaches the scale its proponents are planning for. Whether it closes through deliberate policy or through an incident that makes the gap impossible to ignore is, at this point, an open question.

There is a structural asymmetry built into the current regulatory framework that this transition will eventually force into view. R155 requires vehicle manufacturers to maintain a certified Cybersecurity Management System across the vehicle lifecycle. R156 requires a Software Update Management System for vehicle software. Neither regulation applies to roadside units — they are infrastructure, not vehicles, and they do not go through type approval. NIS2 imposes cybersecurity obligations on critical infrastructure operators, but it is not automotive-specific and does not require anything equivalent to a CSMS in the ISO/SAE 21434 sense.

The result is a trust relationship with a formal asymmetry at its core. The vehicle that receives a V2X message is certified to a standard that includes continuous monitoring, incident response, and supply chain security requirements. The roadside unit that sent it may not be. As V2X transitions from advisory to operational, that asymmetry stops being a theoretical concern and becomes a liability question: when a vehicle acts on a V2X message from infrastructure that was not held to the same standard, and something goes wrong, who is responsible?

That question has no answer in current frameworks. It will need one.

Where manufacturers come from — and what their governments can do

A vehicle contains components from dozens of countries. The ECU might be designed in Germany, fabricated in Taiwan, programmed with software from a US company, using a cellular modem from a Chinese supplier, running an open-source library maintained by a developer in Russia.

Each of those suppliers operates under the laws of their jurisdiction. Those laws may require cooperation with intelligence services. They may permit — or mandate — the insertion of capabilities that would not be disclosed to the OEM or the end customer.

This is not a novel concern. The debate around Huawei's role in 5G infrastructure turned on exactly this question — whether a company subject to Chinese national security law can be trusted with critical communications infrastructure. The automotive supply chain is more complex, more opaque, and less scrutinized than the 5G supply chain.

The specific risk is not necessarily a deliberate backdoor — though that is possible. It is that a supplier operating under a foreign jurisdiction may be legally compelled to provide access to data flowing through their component, or to enable a capability that was not disclosed in the component specification. The OEM may not know. The type approval authority almost certainly does not.

Coordination, interoperability, and the dissolving boundary

The Netherlands has an unusually dense institutional landscape for automotive cybersecurity. What they do not yet do systematically is operate together — and that gap matters, because the threat landscape does not respect organizational mandates.

But the coordination problem does not stop at national borders. A Dutch-registered vehicle driving through Germany, France, and Spain crosses four regulatory jurisdictions. R155 applies throughout — but implementation varies. A V2X message generated by French roadside infrastructure must be interpretable by a vehicle certified under Dutch type approval. An OTA update delivered via a roaming cellular connection crosses multiple network operators and potentially multiple legal jurisdictions simultaneously.

As vehicles become more deeply integrated into shared infrastructure — V2X networks, energy grids, logistics platforms — these overlaps multiply. Who is responsible when a vehicle receives a malicious V2X message from infrastructure in a country where no relevant authority has oversight? Who investigates when a vulnerability is introduced via a foreign cellular network? Who tests a system that spans automotive, aerospace, and logistics simultaneously?

These questions have no answers yet. They point toward something that does not exist: a cross-mobility cybersecurity management framework — shared testing standards, coordinated incident response, and regulatory alignment across jurisdictions and sectors.

The boundary of the connected vehicle is already dissolving. Autonomous delivery drones, last-mile logistics robots, and humanoid platforms like Boston Dynamics or Tesla Optimus are variations on the same architecture — software-defined, network-connected, physically present in the world, and operating in proximity to people. They share attack surfaces, infrastructure dependencies, and in many cases software stacks with passenger vehicles. The cybersecurity engineering challenges are identical: secure boot, authenticated communication, OTA integrity, sensor spoofing resistance, safe fallback behavior.

The frameworks being built for automotive today — ISO/SAE 21434, R155, TARA methodology — are the first iteration of what will eventually need to govern all of these platforms. The institutions that learn to coordinate now, across organizational and national boundaries, will be better positioned than those that wait until the overlap forces them to.

The question is the same one as before: will the coordination happen deliberately, or will it be forced by an incident that made the gap impossible to ignore?

DOK 4 — Extended Thinking

At DOK 3 you reasoned about coordination and governance. At DOK 4 the frame shifts entirely. These passages examine the geopolitical and philosophical dimensions of connected vehicle infrastructure — state-level adversaries, persistent threats at machine scale, and the question of what individual sovereignty means when opting out of connected systems is no longer a realistic option. You are not expected to reach conclusions. You are expected to engage with questions that engineering frameworks alone cannot answer — and to notice that those questions have arrived inside a vehicle cybersecurity masterclass.

Geopolitics and the vehicle as infrastructure

Vehicles are infrastructure. Not metaphorically — literally. They move goods, people, and emergency services. They connect to the power grid. They generate positioning data that maps the movement of populations in real time.

A state that can influence the software running in the vehicle fleet of an adversary nation has a capability that did not exist twenty years ago. That capability does not require kinetic action. It requires a supplier relationship, a software dependency, or a certificate authority in the right position.

The geopolitical implications are being recognized slowly. The US has moved to restrict connected vehicle components from Chinese suppliers on national security grounds. The EU is examining supply chain dependencies in critical infrastructure including automotive. The question of whether a vehicle certified under R155 and built with components from geopolitically contested suppliers is actually secure has no clean answer under current frameworks.

The honest position is that the regulatory frameworks governing vehicle cybersecurity were designed to address commercial security failures — manufacturers who did not invest enough in security. They were not designed to address state-level adversaries with long time horizons and supply chain access. Adapting them for that threat model is a problem that has not been seriously started.

Advanced Persistent Threats in automotive context

An Advanced Persistent Threat is not a smash-and-grab attack. It is a long-term, targeted campaign by a sophisticated actor — typically a nation-state or state-sponsored group — with the patience and resources to establish persistent access and wait.

In automotive context this means: compromise a Tier 2 semiconductor supplier. Insert a capability into a component that ships in millions of vehicles across multiple OEMs. That capability sits dormant, undetected, for years. It activates under specific conditions — a particular geographic location, a particular date, a particular command received over cellular.

This is not science fiction. It is the automotive equivalent of what Stuxnet did to Iranian centrifuges — a targeted, persistent, physically consequential attack delivered through the supply chain. The difference is scale: Stuxnet targeted a specific facility. A compromised automotive component could be present in vehicles across an entire continent.

The detection problem is severe. OEMs do not have full visibility into the firmware running on every component they integrate. Type approval authorities test for compliance, not for the presence of undisclosed capabilities. PTI does not check firmware integrity. There is currently no systematic mechanism for detecting a well-implemented hardware-level backdoor in a production vehicle fleet.

Autonomous adversaries and persistence at machine scale

Most threat models in infrastructure security assume bounded adversaries: organizations with budgets, staffing constraints, operational windows, and human attention cycles. Even state-level actors are ultimately limited by coordination overhead and time.

Systems capable of autonomous operation change that assumption. An automated adversary does not need rest cycles, shift changes, or prioritization tradeoffs in the human sense. It can operate continuously across long time horizons, iterating on partial information, maintaining parallel hypotheses about complex systems, and waiting for conditions that maximize leverage.

In infrastructure terms, this shifts the problem from attack execution to persistent environmental presence — an adversary that does not necessarily strike, but instead observes, models, and adapts over time.

Whether this becomes a dominant security concern depends less on theoretical capability and more on deployment, access, and integration into real-world systems. But it introduces a category of persistence that existing cybersecurity frameworks were not designed to reason about.

The sovereignty question and final thoughts — extended

Individual small choices often reveal larger assumptions about freedom. The person who returns the shopping cart has made a choice. They have traded a small convenience for a contribution to a shared norm. That trade is voluntary. They could choose otherwise.

Notably, one difference separates the connected vehicle from the shopping cart. The driver of a modern connected vehicle has not made that choice in quite the same way. The data collection was in the terms of service. The remote disable capability was part of the type approval framework. Mandatory ADAS systems were required by regulation before the vehicle reached the showroom. The driver accepted all of it by purchasing the vehicle — but the practical alternatives were increasingly limited: a used car with minimal connectivity, or no car at all.

Defining voluntary participation becomes harder when alternatives disappear. This is the shape of the problem. Individual sovereignty — the classical liberal principle that a person is the primary authority over themselves and their property — depends on the existence of meaningful alternatives. When regulatory and commercial systems progressively narrow those alternatives, the formal existence of choice may no longer feel entirely equivalent to meaningful consent.

Emerging as perhaps the clearest current example of this dynamic, the vehicle is unlikely to be the last. The same architecture — software-defined, remotely managed, continuously monitored, and increasingly standardized — is appearing in homes, workplaces, and the digital platforms that mediate much of modern social and economic life.

Perhaps, the question is not whether connected vehicles are worth their trade-offs. For most people, in most contexts, they probably are. The more difficult question is how societies decide when the benefits of technology become closely tied to participation in ordinary life, and how much influence individuals retain over those decisions.

Engineering alone cannot fully answer these questions. It belongs, at least in part, to political philosophy. The fact that it has emerged inside a vehicle cybersecurity masterclass is itself worth noticing.

Nineteen ninety-six saw John Perry Barlow publish A Declaration of the Independence of Cyberspace, a manifesto addressed to the governments of the industrial world. Its opening line was famously defiant: "Governments of the Industrial World, you weary giants of flesh and steel, I come from Cyberspace, the new home of Mind. On behalf of the future, I ask you of the past to leave us alone."

Decades later, Barlow’s declaration was revealed to be idealistic, utopian, and ultimately inaccurate as prediction. Cyberspace did not remain ungoverned. States asserted jurisdiction. Infrastructure centralized. Platforms evolved under a combination of commercial incentives, technical constraints, and regulatory pressure.

Even so, the tension Barlow identified was real. The question of who governs connected systems — and by what legitimacy — did not resolve itself in favor of either pure state control or pure individual freedom. Instead, it became an ongoing negotiation between governments, corporations, technical standards bodies, and users themselves.

Now the connected vehicle sits inside that negotiation. UNECE R155 represents a state asserting jurisdiction over vehicle software. Remote disable capability reflects manufacturers retaining some degree of operational control after sale. Continuous telemetry collection reflects the commercial value of behavioral data generated in private environments. The driver — despite being the person most directly affected — has relatively limited influence over how these systems evolve once regulatory, technical, and commercial standards converge.

Clearly, you could argue that Barlow was wrong that cyberspace would remain independent from institutional control. He was right that the question of who governs connected systems, and on whose authority, would become increasingly important.

Eventually, thirty years later, that question has arrived in the vehicle in your driveway. And unlike the shopping cart dilemma, opting out is becoming increasingly difficult.
